PATIENT SAFETY CONFERENCE

Delayed Ophthalmology Evaluation

A Case Study in Diagnostic Delay, Systems Failure, and the Critical Importance of In-Person Specialist Evaluation

⏱️ Clinical Timeline

The sequence of events revealing multiple missed opportunities for earlier diagnosis

Day 0 - Initial Presentation
Hospital Admission
54-year-old male presents with left-sided vision impairment, significant eyelid swelling, and rhabdomyolysis. IV antibiotics started for empiric coverage of orbital cellulitis despite CT/MRI head not initially supporting diagnosis.
Day 1 - Morning
Complete Vision Loss ⚠️
Patient endorses complete loss of left-sided vision, raising urgent concern for orbital cellulitis. Ophthalmology consulted.
Day 1 - Ophthalmology Response #1
Telephone Recommendation Only
Contact made with "Osiris" (ophthalmic assistant, per Google search). Recommended continuing IV antibiotics for 48 hours and reconsult if no improvement. Stated complete vision loss can occur due to swelling. Provided oral antibiotics, eye drops, and outpatient follow-up information. Declined to see patient before 48 hours elapsed.
Day 2-3
Partial Clinical Improvement
Eyelid swelling improved, but vision did not return. Patient began experiencing pain, prompting re-consultation.
Day 3 - Ophthalmology Response #2
Second Telephone Recommendation
Ophthalmology again stated complete vision loss can occur due to swelling and clinical course is "expected." Recommended outpatient follow-up despite patient still admitted for rhabdomyolysis. Still no in-person evaluation.
Day 3-4
Additional Imaging Ordered
MRI orbit ordered due to increasing pain with eye movement to rule out abscess. Results showed concern for left orbital cellulitis AND optic neuritis.
Day 4 - Ophthalmology Response #3
Request for Photos via Cerner
Vision loss persisted. Ophthalmology re-consulted. Requested uploading pictures of the eye to Cerner electronic system rather than in-person evaluation.
Day 4 - Attending Advocacy
Escalation Required
Attending requested in-person evaluation given preceding events. Doc-to-doc conversation with ophthalmologist. Initial response: "If there's no abscess, nothing else to do besides IV antibiotics." Attending advocated strongly, noting significance of visual loss and concern about missing pathology team not trained to evaluate. Ophthalmologist finally agreed to see patient.
Day 4 - In-Person Evaluation
Diagnosis: Central Retinal Artery Occlusion
Ophthalmologist finally evaluated patient in-person and determined diagnosis of central retinal artery occlusion (CRAO). Recommended stroke workup, largely already completed except echocardiogram, which would further prolong hospital stay for patient who had already attempted to leave AMA several times.

🔍 Root Cause Analysis

Fishbone diagram identifying contributing factors across people, environment, processes, methods, and technology

PEOPLE
  • Consultant resistant to in-person evaluation
  • Residents/team uncomfortable with thorough ophthalmologic exam
  • Answering service staffed by non-physician
ENVIRONMENT
  • Weekend timing
  • Lack of academic ophthalmology service
  • No in-house ophthalmology coverage
  • Limited MRI availability delayed imaging
  • Deference to consultant expertise
PROCESSES
  • No policy requiring in-person specialist evaluation
  • No escalation pathway for declined consults
  • Unclear criteria for urgent vs. emergent consults
METHODS
  • Callbook system for finding consultants
  • "Consult Physician" order in Cerner
  • Reliance on answering services
TECHNOLOGY
  • No direct communication with consultant
  • Only indirect contact via answering service
  • Photo upload system used as substitute for evaluation
Adverse Outcome
DELAYED DIAGNOSIS OF CRAO

🎯 Contributing Factors Summary

Primary Systems Failures

  • No mandatory in-person evaluation policy for vision-threatening emergencies
  • No escalation pathway when specialists decline appropriate evaluation
  • Communication barrier — inability to communicate directly with ophthalmologist
  • Non-physician intermediary making clinical triage decisions

Secondary Contributing Factors

  • Weekend timing with reduced specialist availability
  • Community hospital without academic ophthalmology service
  • Limited MRI scanner availability delaying diagnostic imaging
  • Team deference to specialist expertise, even when recommendations seemed inappropriate
  • Resident team lacking confidence/training in ophthalmologic examination

🧠 Cognitive Biases Identified

Understanding the cognitive errors that contributed to diagnostic delay

Anchoring Bias
The tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information encountered (the "anchor") when making decisions, even when that information is incomplete or incorrect.
How it manifested: Early fixation on orbital cellulitis as the diagnosis, despite negative initial imaging (CT and MRI head). The team and consultant remained anchored to this diagnosis even as the clinical picture evolved and vision loss became complete, delaying consideration of alternative etiologies like CRAO.
Premature Closure
The tendency to stop considering alternative diagnoses once an initial diagnosis is reached, failing to reconsider when new information emerges.
How it manifested: After settling on orbital cellulitis, there was failure to reconsider the diagnosis despite worsening vision, development of pain, and new MRI findings suggesting optic neuritis. The diagnostic process "closed" prematurely, preventing timely recognition of CRAO.
Authority Bias
The tendency to attribute greater accuracy to the opinion of an authority figure and be more influenced by that opinion, even when the authority's recommendations may be inappropriate.
How it manifested: Excessive deference to ophthalmology consultant recommendations (via answering service) despite multiple red flags: complete vision loss, persistent symptoms, evolving clinical picture. The team hesitated to push back against specialist recommendations even when in-person evaluation seemed clearly indicated.
Availability Bias
The tendency to overestimate the likelihood of events that are more memorable or easier to recall, often because they are dramatic, recent, or frequently discussed.
How it manifested: Orbital cellulitis is a commonly taught cause of vision loss with eyelid swelling, making it readily "available" for consideration. CRAO, while time-critical, may have been less readily considered due to lower frequency in general practice, despite being the correct diagnosis.
💡
Debiasing Strategy

Forced Consideration of Alternatives: In cases of acute, complete vision loss, implement a systematic differential diagnosis review that explicitly includes vascular etiologies (CRAO, CRVO), inflammatory causes (optic neuritis), and infectious etiologies. This checklist-based approach can counteract anchoring and premature closure.

✅ Practice Improvement Recommendations

System-level changes to prevent similar adverse events

1 Mandatory In-Person Ophthalmology Evaluation Policy
Recommendation: Establish institutional policy requiring mandatory in-person ophthalmology consultation within 2 hours for all cases of new, acute, or worsening vision loss.

Rationale: Vision loss is a time-sensitive emergency. CRAO has a therapeutic window similar to stroke (hours, not days). Remote assessment via photos or telephonic recommendations is insufficient for acute vision-threatening conditions.

Implementation: Create clear criteria in EHR order sets. Flag "acute vision loss" consults as requiring immediate in-person evaluation. Provide explicit language for requesting physicians to escalate if declined.
2 Consultation Escalation Pathway
Recommendation: Develop a formal escalation pathway when specialists decline what requesting team considers appropriate evaluation. Include chain of command (consultant → division chief → department chair → CMO).

Rationale: Currently, no mechanism exists for residents/attendings to escalate when specialist recommendations seem inappropriate. This creates dangerous power imbalances where urgent patient needs may not be met.

Implementation: Create consultation escalation protocol. Train residents on appropriate use. Track escalation events for quality review.
3 Direct Physician Communication for Urgent Consults
Recommendation: Require direct physician-to-physician communication for all urgent/emergent consultations, eliminating non-physician answering services as intermediaries for clinical decision-making.

Rationale: Clinical triage decisions made by non-physicians (even trained ophthalmic assistants) may not adequately assess urgency or recognize red flags requiring immediate evaluation.

Implementation: Provide direct contact numbers for on-call specialists. Mandate callback within 15 minutes for urgent consults. Consider secure messaging platforms for rapid physician-to-physician communication.
4 Enhanced Diagnostic Imaging Availability
Recommendation: Increase MRI scanner availability and prioritize urgent neurologic/ophthalmologic cases to reduce time to diagnosis.

Rationale: Delayed MRI orbit availability in this case prolonged diagnostic uncertainty and contributed to delayed recognition of optic neuritis and ultimately CRAO.

Implementation: Assess current MRI utilization patterns. Consider additional scanner or extended hours. Create clear prioritization criteria for urgent ophthalmologic/neurologic imaging.
5 Resident Education: Acute Vision Loss Evaluation
Recommendation: Provide structured teaching sessions on basic ophthalmologic examination and differential diagnosis of acute vision loss, empowering residents to better recognize urgent pathology and advocate for appropriate specialist evaluation.

Rationale: While specialist consultation is essential, residents should have baseline competency to recognize vision-threatening emergencies and understand when specialist evaluation is non-negotiable.

Implementation: Include acute vision loss module in resident orientation. Provide pocket cards with red flags requiring immediate ophthalmology evaluation. Simulation cases during morning report.

🎯 Implementation Priorities

  • Immediate (0-3 months): Establish consultation escalation pathway; create acute vision loss policy
  • Short-term (3-6 months): Implement direct physician communication requirement; develop resident education module
  • Long-term (6-12 months): Assess MRI capacity and consider expansion; track outcomes and policy compliance
⚡
Key Principle: Systems Over Individuals

These recommendations focus on process and environmental improvements rather than individual education or blame. Healthcare errors are nearly always systems failures, not individual competence issues. Effective patient safety interventions redesign systems to make errors impossible or immediately obvious.

📋 Case Overview

A 54-year-old male presented to the hospital with impaired left-sided vision and significant eyelid swelling. Despite complete vision loss developing during admission and persistent symptoms, multiple barriers to in-person ophthalmology evaluation led to a critical diagnostic delay of a central retinal artery occlusion (CRAO) — a time-sensitive ophthalmologic emergency.

🏥 Clinical Presentation

Initial Presentation

  • Left-sided vision impairment with significant eyelid swelling
  • Patient uncertain if impairment was from swelling vs. intrinsic eye pathology
  • Concurrent rhabdomyolysis (stable)
  • CT and MRI head initially not supportive of orbital cellulitis despite clinical suspicion

Disease Progression

  • Morning after admission: Complete loss of left-sided vision
  • Mid-course: Eyelid swelling improved but vision remained absent
  • Later course: Development of pain with eye movement
  • MRI orbit: Concern for orbital cellulitis AND optic neuritis

Final Diagnosis

Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO) — A stroke of the eye requiring immediate intervention, similar to acute ischemic stroke requiring intervention within hours for optimal outcomes.

🎯 Key Learning Points

  • Complete, acute vision loss is an ophthalmologic emergency requiring immediate specialist evaluation
  • Consultant recommendations via non-physician intermediaries may not reflect full clinical urgency
  • Systems must support escalation pathways when specialists decline appropriate evaluation
  • Anchoring on initial diagnoses (orbital cellulitis) can delay recognition of alternative pathologies (CRAO)
